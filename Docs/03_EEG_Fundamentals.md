# Electroencephalography (EEG) Fundamentals

---

# 1. Introduction

Electroencephalography (EEG) is a non-invasive technique used to measure the electrical activity of the brain.

Whenever neurons in the brain communicate with each other, tiny electrical signals are generated. EEG records these electrical signals using electrodes placed on the scalp.

In simple words,

> **EEG records the brain's electrical activity as voltage signals over time.**

---

# 2. Why Are We Using EEG?

Several technologies can measure brain activity, such as:

- EEG
- fMRI
- MEG
- ECoG

This project uses **EEG** because:

- It is completely non-invasive.
- It is safe and portable.
- It provides excellent temporal resolution.
- Public datasets are easily available.
- It is widely used in SSVEP-based Brain-Computer Interfaces.
- It is suitable for real-time applications.

Therefore, EEG is an ideal choice for our SSVEP-based color encoding project.

---

# 3. What Does an EEG Machine Measure?

An EEG machine measures:

> **Voltage changes generated by brain activity over time.**

It does **not** directly measure:

- Thoughts
- Images
- Colors
- Memories

Instead, it records electrical signals produced by millions of neurons.

The recorded data looks like:

| Time (s) | Voltage (µV) |
|-----------|--------------|
| 0.000 | 1.2 |
| 0.004 | 0.9 |
| 0.008 | 1.5 |
| ... | ... |

Therefore, EEG data is fundamentally:

```text
Voltage vs Time
```

---

# 4. Electrodes and Channels

## Electrodes

Electrodes are small sensors placed on the scalp that detect electrical activity generated by the brain.

---

## Channels

A channel represents one recorded EEG signal.

For example,

```text
64 Electrodes
        ↓
64 Channels
        ↓
64 Different Voltage Signals
```

Each channel records the electrical activity from a different region of the brain.

---

# 5. Why Do We Need Multiple Channels?

Different parts of the brain perform different functions.

Our project focuses on **visual processing**.

Visual information is processed in the:

```text
Occipital Lobe
        ↓
Visual Cortex
```

Therefore, channels located near the occipital region are the most important.

Examples:

- O1
- O2
- Oz

These channels are commonly used in SSVEP-based Brain-Computer Interfaces.

---

# 6. Sampling Frequency

An EEG machine records voltage values many times every second.

The number of measurements taken per second is called the **Sampling Frequency**.

It is measured in:

```text
Hertz (Hz)
```

Example:

```text
250 Hz
```

means:

```text
250 voltage measurements per second.
```

Other common sampling frequencies include:

- 250 Hz
- 500 Hz
- 1000 Hz

Higher sampling frequencies produce more detailed recordings but also generate larger datasets.

---

# 7. Understanding EEG Data

An EEG recording can be viewed as a collection of voltage values.

One channel:

```text
[v1, v2, v3, v4, ...]
```

Multiple channels:

```text
[
 Channel1,
 Channel2,
 Channel3,
 ...
 Channel64
]
```

Thus, EEG data is essentially a matrix of voltage values.

---

# 8. Example for Our Project

Suppose an EEG machine records:

- Duration = 8 seconds
- Sampling Frequency = 250 Hz
- Channels = 64

### Samples in one channel

```text
8 × 250 = 2000 samples
```

### Total recorded voltage values

```text
2000 × 64 = 128000 values
```

This means that a very large amount of data is generated even from a short EEG recording.

---

# 9. EEG Waves

The brain naturally produces different rhythmic activities called **brain waves**.

| Wave | Frequency Range |
|------|----------------|
| Delta | 0.5 – 4 Hz |
| Theta | 4 – 8 Hz |
| Alpha | 8 – 13 Hz |
| Beta | 13 – 30 Hz |
| Gamma | Above 30 Hz |

These waves represent natural brain activity.

It is important to note that:

> **EEG waves are different from SSVEP signals.**

SSVEP signals are generated specifically when a person observes a flickering visual stimulus.

# 10. EEG in Our Project

Our project uses EEG to record brain responses generated by visual stimulation.

The workflow is:

```text
Light Flicker
      ↓
Human Eye
      ↓
Visual Cortex
      ↓
Electrical Activity
      ↓
EEG Recording
      ↓
Voltage Signals
      ↓
Signal Processing
      ↓
Frequency Detection
      ↓
Color Encoding
```

EEG therefore acts as the primary source of information for the entire system.

---

# 11. Key Takeaways

- EEG records electrical activity from the brain.
- EEG measures voltage changes over time.
- Electrodes record signals from different brain regions.
- Multiple channels provide information from different parts of the brain.
- O1, O2, and Oz are particularly important for SSVEP applications.
- Sampling frequency determines how much data is recorded.
- EEG provides the raw signals that will later be processed using FFT, CCA, FBCCA, and other algorithms.

# Summary

Electroencephalography (EEG) is a non-invasive technique for measuring brain activity.

For this project, EEG is used to record the brain's response to visual flickering stimuli.
